History and Legal Background

Before the introduction of legal parameters that ensured accessibility, the topic of disability and the experiences of those with disabilities. The film Crip Camp (which is available for streaming on Netflix) describes the experiences of those living with disabilities before the introduction of legal standards of accessibility. As the film points out, the issue was not building accessible infrastructure; rather, the majority of the push towards accessibility rested on convincing government officials to invest the time and money to change existing infrastructure. Even after laws such as Section 508 of the US Rehabilitation Act (1978) were introduced, activists faced considerable resistance from government officials who were unwilling to invest the time and effort to restructure existing infrastructure or design new infrastructure with accessibility in mind.

While laws addressing accessibility vary, the main laws and guidelines that you should refer to in the United States are:

Section 508, The U.S. Rehabilitation Act (1978): This landmark amendment to the United States Rehabilitation Act states that all U.S. federal employees with disabilities have equal access to information. It also requires that all information shared by the U.S. federal agencies be accessible to those with disabilities. This amendment was refreshed in 2017 to include online resources and recommends that sites follow WCAG (see below) level A and level AA guidelines at a minimum.

Americans with Disabilities Act: The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), which was passed in 1990, requires that all businesses open to the public and all government agencies must meet accessibility requirements. While this does not pertain to all businesses, most stores, banks, food establishments, and those in the hospitality, medical, and entertainment industries are legally required to follow the standards set in the Americans with Disabilities Act.

WCAG: Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are the international standard for accessibility. These provide an excellent overview of the best practices to ensure that online resources remain accessible, which are applicable to all forms of online resources—including video and audio resources. WCAG criteria are split into three levels: WCAG level A (the lowest level of conformance), level AA (the middle level of conformance, which satisfies level A and AA requirements), and level AAA (the highest level of conformance that satisfies the requirements of all three).

ATAG: The Authoring Tool Accessibility Guidelines (ATAG). These guidelines will help you “ensure that the authoring element of the site is accessible” (Kalbag, ch. 7), and that “the authoring element helps users produce accessible content” (Kalbag, ch. 7). Much like WCAG, ATAG guidelines have levels of conformance (level A, AA, and AAA).

Court Cases: Companies have been sued in the past for either refusing to comply with accessibility guidelines and laws or for improper compliance. Past court cases have set a firm legal precedent that could cost companies thousands (even millions) of dollars in class-action lawsuits and certifications. An example of this is the National Federation of the Blind v. Target Corporation (2006), where the ruling stated that all commercial websites must be accessible under ADA and state law (Kalbag, ch. 7).
